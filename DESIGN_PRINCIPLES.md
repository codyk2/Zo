# Design Principles — Agent Reference

> **For AI agents working on EMPIRE.** When you're making a UI, layout, copy, or UX decision and want grounding in evidence-based design, open this file first. It is an index into *Universal Principles of Design* (Lidwell, Holden, Butler, Rockport Publishers, 2003) — the 100-principle cross-disciplinary design reference bound to the repo.

## Source PDF

[`william-lidwell-kritina-holden-jill-butler-universal-principles-of-design-rockport-publishers-2003 (1).pdf`](./william-lidwell-kritina-holden-jill-butler-universal-principles-of-design-rockport-publishers-2003%20(1).pdf) at the repo root. 216 book pages / 109 PDF pages. Each principle gets a two-page spread (left = definition + guidelines, right = diagrams/examples).

Page numbers below are **book pages** (what the TOC prints). In most PDF viewers, "Go to page" accepts these directly. If not, approximate PDF page ≈ `(book_page / 2) + 2`.

## How to use

1. **Identify the design question you're solving.** Scan the "Quick lookup by question" section to find the relevant cluster of principles.
2. **Open the PDF at the cited page.** Read the left-hand page (~300 words) for the principle and its application guidelines. Look at the right-hand page for worked examples.
3. **Cite the principle by name in your reasoning.** When you recommend something, say *why* — "favor Recognition Over Recall (p164) for the comment-type selector" — so the user can push back on the principle, not just the output.
4. **Treat it as a checklist, not a constraint.** The book's own guidance: good designers sometimes violate principles on purpose, but they pay a cost when they do. If you violate, say so explicitly.

## Full A–Z index

Each entry: **Principle** (page) — one-line gist. "See also" relationships are in the book itself.

### Numbers
- **80/20 Rule** (12) — ~80% of effects come from ~20% of causes; focus design effort on the critical 20% of features.

### A
- **Accessibility** (14) — Design for the widest range of users without modification: perceptibility, operability, simplicity, forgiveness.
- **Advance Organizer** (16) — Give a brief mental scaffold (big picture) before detail, so new info attaches to what users already know.
- **Aesthetic-Usability Effect** (18) — Attractive designs are perceived as easier to use, and users are more tolerant of their flaws.
- **Affordance** (20) — Physical characteristics of an object suggest how to use it; match affordance to intended action.
- **Alignment** (22) — Place elements on common lines/edges; improves aesthetics, order, and perceived unity.
- **Archetypes** (24) — Universally recognized symbols/characters/patterns that tap into shared human experience.
- **Attractiveness Bias** (26) — People attribute positive traits to attractive people; generalize to designs.
- **B**
- **Baby-Face Bias** (28) — Round features and big eyes evoke naivety and warmth; use deliberately.

### C
- **Chunking** (30) — Group info into units of ~4 ± 1 so short-term memory can hold them.
- **Classical Conditioning** (32) — Pair a stimulus with a response until the stimulus alone triggers it (brand-feeling associations).
- **Closure** (34) — The mind completes incomplete shapes; use gaps to imply forms with less ink.
- **Cognitive Dissonance** (36) — People resolve conflict between action and belief; design to align user effort with identity.
- **Color** (38) — Attracts attention, groups elements, symbolizes meaning; use sparingly and with sensitivity to colorblindness.
- **Common Fate** (40) — Elements moving together are perceived as related (Gestalt).
- **Comparison** (42) — Put related values side by side using a common scale for honest visual comparison.
- **Confirmation** (44) — Require verification of intent before high-consequence actions (delete, buy).
- **Consistency** (46) — Similar parts behave/look similar; accelerates learning and reduces errors.
- **Constancy** (48) — Perception stays stable under viewpoint/lighting/size changes — leverage for recognizable UI.
- **Constraint** (50) — Restrict possible actions to make correct use easy and errors hard.
- **Control** (52) — Match controls to the user's skill level; experts need power, novices need guardrails.
- **Convergence** (54) — Competing designs converge on successful forms over time — watch peers.
- **Cost-Benefit** (56) — An element earns its place only when benefit exceeds its cognitive/financial cost.

### D
- **Defensible Space** (58) — Environments that give clear territorial cues feel safer and get cared for.
- **Depth of Processing** (60) — Info processed deeply (understood) is remembered better than info processed shallowly (rehearsed).
- **Development Cycle** (62) — Every design goes through requirements → design → development → testing; skip a stage at your peril.

### E
- **Entry Point** (64) — Design a clear, low-commitment starting place that draws people in.
- **Errors** (66) — Two kinds: slips (wrong action, right intent) and mistakes (wrong intent). Design against each differently.
- **Expectation Effect** (68) — People experience what they expect; prime expectations to shape perception.
- **Exposure Effect** (70) — Repeated exposure to a neutral stimulus increases preference for it (within limits).

### F
- **Face-ism Ratio** (72) — Proportion of face-to-body in an image shifts perception of intelligence (more face) vs. physicality (less).
- **Factor of Safety** (74) — Build in margin beyond the expected worst case; user/environmental variance exceeds estimates.
- **Feedback Loop** (76) — Systems self-regulate via positive (amplifying) or negative (dampening) feedback; UIs need the loop to close.
- **Fibonacci Sequence** (78) — Numeric pattern (1,1,2,3,5,8,13…) recurring in nature; underpins aesthetic proportions. Closely tied to Golden Ratio.
- **Figure-Ground Relationship** (80) — Perception separates scenes into figure (focus) and ground (rest); design clear figure-ground or lose attention.
- **Fitts' Law** (82) — Time to hit a target scales with distance and inversely with target size. Put important controls big and near; use screen edges.
- **Five Hat Racks** (84) — Only five ways to organize info: category, time, location, alphabet, continuum (LATCH).
- **Flexibility-Usability Tradeoff** (86) — More flexibility = more complexity = less usability. Favor specialized designs when user needs are well-defined.
- **Forgiveness** (88) — Prevent errors (affordances, constraints), allow reversal (undo), provide safety nets.
- **Form Follows Function** (90) — Aesthetics serve functional success criteria. Define success first, then let form follow.
- **Framing** (92) — How info is presented (positive vs. negative) shifts decisions even when content is identical. Present products in a positive frame.

### G
- **Garbage In–Garbage Out** (94) — Output quality is capped by input quality; use affordances, constraints, previews, validation.
- **Golden Ratio** (96) — Ratio ≈ 1.618; recurring in nature and admired compositions. A useful proportion, not a rule.
- **Good Continuation** (98) — Elements on a continuous line/curve are perceived as grouped (Gestalt). Align related items; break lines to disassociate.
- **Gutenberg Diagram** (100) — Left-to-right readers scan: primary optical area (top-left) → strong fallow (top-right) → weak fallow (bottom-left) → terminal area (bottom-right). Place CTAs in the terminal area.

### H
- **Hick's Law** (102) — Decision time scales with log(N choices). Fewer options = faster decisions. Chunk long menus.
- **Hierarchy** (104) — Tree, nest, or stair structures clarify rank and relationship; every complex system needs one.
- **Hierarchy of Needs** (106) — Maslow-style: designs must satisfy lower needs (functional, reliable, usable) before higher (proficient, creative).
- **Highlighting** (108) — Emphasis technique (bold, color, motion) — apply to ≤10% of content or the effect dies.

### I
- **Iconic Representation** (110) — Pictures communicate faster than words for universal concepts. Four types: similar, example, symbolic, arbitrary.
- **Immersion** (112) — Deep engagement mental state (flow). Match challenge to skill; remove distractions.
- **Interference Effects** (114) — Conflicting stimuli (e.g., Stroop) slow processing. Avoid clashing cues.
- **Inverted Pyramid** (116) — Put conclusion first, then supporting detail, then background. News-article structure.
- **Iteration** (118) — Design improves through repeated build-test-refine cycles; one-shot design always fails.

### L
- **Law of Prägnanz** (120) — The perceptual system organizes input toward the simplest possible interpretation.
- **Layering** (122) — Stack info in related groups; two kinds: two-dimensional (zoom/drill) and three-dimensional (overlay).
- **Legibility** (124) — Visual clarity of characters; depends on typeface, size, contrast, spacing.
- **Life Cycle** (126) — Four stages (introduction, growth, maturity, decline). Design decisions vary by stage.

### M
- **Mapping** (128) — Spatial/semantic correspondence between controls and what they control (stove burners ↔ knobs).
- **Mental Model** (130) — Users' internal simulation of how a system works. Match your system model to theirs or explain the gap.
- **Mimicry** (132) — Copy elements of familiar objects to communicate function or feeling. Three types: surface, behavioral, functional.
- **Mnemonic Device** (134) — Memory aid (acronym, rhyme, peg). Useful for onboarding terminology.
- **Modularity** (136) — Decompose a system into smaller independent parts; enables scaling, testing, reuse.
- **Most Average Facial Appearance Effect** (138) — Averaged faces are rated more attractive than any individual face. Applies broadly to design averaging.

### N
- **Normal Distribution** (140) — Natural variance follows a bell curve. Design for the 68% in the middle, accommodate the tails.

### O
- **Ockham's Razor** (142) — Given equal alternatives, prefer the simplest. Remove anything that doesn't earn its place.
- **Operant Conditioning** (144) — Behavior shaped by reinforcement (positive/negative). Reward the actions you want users to repeat.
- **Orientation Sensitivity** (146) — Horizontal/vertical lines are detected fastest; diagonals slower. Align to cardinal axes by default.

### P
- **Performance Load** (148) — Mental + physical effort required to achieve a goal. Lower both; chunk, automate, default.
- **Performance Versus Preference** (150) — Users prefer ≠ users perform best on. Measure both; design for performance, tune for preference.
- **Picture Superiority Effect** (152) — Pictures are remembered better than words. Pair images with labels for durable recall.
- **Progressive Disclosure** (154) — Reveal complexity only as needed. Keep primary flows minimal; expose advanced options on demand.
- **Prospect-Refuge** (156) — People prefer spaces offering both a view out (prospect) and shelter (refuge). Translates to dashboards: overview + focus.
- **Prototyping** (158) — Low-fidelity (paper/wireframe) → high-fidelity. Match prototype fidelity to the question being answered.
- **Proximity** (160) — Elements placed near each other are perceived as related (Gestalt). Spacing is grouping.

### R
- **Readability** (162) — How easily text can be understood; depends on sentence structure, word choice, document layout. Distinct from legibility.
- **Recognition Over Recall** (164) — Show options (menus, thumbnails) rather than asking users to remember them (blank fields).
- **Redundancy** (166) — Duplicate critical components/cues so a single failure doesn't break the system (same as Factor of Safety but at the element level).
- **Rule of Thirds** (168) — Compose on a 3×3 grid; place subjects on the thirds lines or intersections.

### S
- **Satisficing** (170) — Users pick the first acceptable option, not the best. Put good-enough defaults up front.
- **Savanna Preference** (172) — Humans prefer open grassy vistas with scattered trees and water; a deep aesthetic pull.
- **Scaling Fallacy** (174) — Systems don't scale linearly; ×10 users ≠ ×10 of the same architecture.
- **Self-Similarity** (176) — Patterns that repeat at multiple scales (fractals). Pleasing; also aids structural efficiency.
- **Serial Position Effects** (178) — Items at the start (primacy) and end (recency) of a list are remembered better than the middle. Put critical items there.
- **Shaping** (180) — Teach complex behavior by rewarding successive approximations. Onboard incrementally.
- **Signal-to-Noise Ratio** (182) — Maximize information (signal); minimize decoration and irrelevant elements (noise).
- **Similarity** (184) — Similar-looking elements are perceived as related (Gestalt). Opposite use of Similarity: introduce variation to disassociate.
- **Storytelling** (186) — Narrative structure (setup → conflict → resolution) engages and is remembered better than bare facts.
- **Structural Forms** (188) — Mass (solid), frame (skeletal), surface (shell) — each carries loads differently. Applies to info architecture too.
- **Symmetry** (190) — Bilateral, radial, translational. Suggests order and stability; asymmetry suggests dynamism.

### T
- **Threat Detection** (192) — Humans detect snakes/spiders/angry faces faster than neutral stimuli. Use sparingly to attract attention.
- **Three-Dimensional Projection** (194) — Methods to render depth on 2D (perspective, shadow, overlap). Pick the minimum that works.
- **Top-Down Lighting Bias** (196) — Brains assume light comes from above. Shadows under elements read as "raised"; above as "sunken."

### U
- **Uncertainty Principle** (198) — Observing a system changes it (user studies included). Compensate with multiple methods.
- **Uniform Connectedness** (200) — Elements connected by a shared region or line are perceived as grouped (Gestalt). Use enclosures/dividers intentionally.

### V
- **Visibility** (202) — System status should be visible; users should always know what state they're in and what's possible next.
- **von Restorff Effect** (204) — An item that differs from its neighbors stands out and is remembered. Use for the one thing that matters.

### W
- **Waist-to-Hip Ratio** (206) — Biological beauty cue (~0.7 for women, ~0.9 for men). Applies broadly to proportional preference.
- **Wayfinding** (208) — Orientation + navigation in complex spaces. Four parts: orientation, route decision, route monitoring, destination recognition.
- **Weakest Link** (210) — A system is only as strong as its weakest component. Find it, strengthen it, or remove it.

## Citing this resource in your reasoning

When you make a design recommendation, cite the principle by name and page so the user can verify:

> "I'd move the `$saved` KPI to the top-right corner of RoutingPanel — Gutenberg Diagram (p100) puts the strong fallow area there, and judges scan left-to-right, top-to-bottom. Keeping % local in the top-left honors the primary optical area."

> "The 'Reading…' chip should stay visible until `comment_response_video` *or* `comment_failed` fires — Visibility (p202) and Feedback Loop (p76). A silent failure is the worst UX option."

When you intentionally violate a principle, name the tradeoff:

> "I'm violating Ockham's Razor (p142) by keeping both the sell text input and the VoiceMic — the duplication earns its place because voice is the demo's headline, but typed fallback is the judge's escape hatch."
